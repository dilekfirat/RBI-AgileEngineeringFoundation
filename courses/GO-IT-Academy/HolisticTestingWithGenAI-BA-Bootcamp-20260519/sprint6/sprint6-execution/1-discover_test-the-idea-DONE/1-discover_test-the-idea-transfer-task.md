# Transfer Task

## Title

Validate a real “expand into a new market” idea with evidence (not a slide-deck opinion)

## Learning Objective

After completing this task, you can frame a narrow, testable market hypothesis for a product or channel you care about, gather **primary or clearly sourced secondary evidence** (not generic industry paragraphs), and decide whether the idea deserves a next step—or a stop—based on what you found.

## Context

In the sprint hands-on task, you explored whether expanding a **webshop into the Czech Republic** could make business sense: real market evidence, your own questions, and scope you chose (products, competitors, marketplaces, risks, opportunities).

A **transfer task** applies the same discipline in **your** work context: another country, another customer segment, a new sales channel, or a partner ecosystem—where “sounds good in a meeting” is cheap, but **wrong bets** are expensive.

## Task Description

1. **Pick one expansion or pivot idea** you can actually influence within the next quarter (e.g. new geography, new vertical, marketplace listing, B2B vs B2C trial). Write it in one sentence as a **decision question** (e.g. “Should we prioritize X in market Y for product Z?”).

2. **Define your own scope** (mirroring the hands-on task, but for *your* idea). Explicitly choose:
   - which **products or offers** you analyze (narrow: one family or SKU group, not “everything we sell”),
   - which **competitors or substitutes** you investigate (name at least three concrete players or listings),
   - which **marketplaces or discovery surfaces** matter (where buyers actually look),
   - which **risks** you want to validate (e.g. logistics, regulation, price position, returns, trust signals),
   - which **opportunities** you want to explore (e.g. gap in assortment, weak competitor reviews, channel margin).

3. **Collect evidence** for at least **five** distinct “signals.” Examples of signals (pick what fits your idea): public price checks, shipping/return policy comparison, competitor review themes, search demand proxies, one structured interview or internal data snippet (non-confidential), regulatory checklist item with official source link, or marketplace fee/margin back-of-envelope. **Do not** rely only on generic analyst PDFs or uncited LLM summaries—tie each signal to a **source you could show a colleague**.

4. **Answer your decision question** in half a page: **Go / No-go / More discovery**, with three bullet reasons grounded in your signals. If evidence is thin, say what you would measure next and in what timeframe.

5. **Optional but valuable:** One paragraph on **what would falsify** your conclusion (what new evidence would change your mind).

## Expected Outcome

One markdown file (name per your team convention, e.g. `transfer-market-discovery-<topic>.md`) containing:

- The decision question in one sentence  
- Your self-defined scope (products, competitors, surfaces, risks, opportunities)  
- At least five labeled signals with sources  
- Go / No-go / More discovery with three evidence-based reasons  
- Optional falsification paragraph  

## Constraints

- **No purely theoretical essay**; every main claim should trace to a named source, screenshot description, quote with link, or first-hand observation you document.  
- **Protect confidentiality**; do not paste customer names, unreleased figures, or internal-only strategy in full—summarize safely.  
- **Time-box** evidence gathering to what you can complete in the estimated duration; prefer depth on a narrow scope over breadth with no proof.  

## Acceptance Criteria

- [ ] Decision question is single-sentence and testable.  
- [ ] Scope lists concrete products, ≥3 competitors/substitutes, and at least one marketplace or discovery surface.  
- [ ] ≥5 signals, each with an identifiable source (link, document title + date, or “internal: role/date” without secrets).  
- [ ] Verdict is one of Go / No-go / More discovery, with exactly three bullets tied to specific signals.  
- [ ] At least one risk and one opportunity from your scope list is explicitly addressed in the evidence or verdict.  

## Hints

- Start from **buyer behavior** (where they search, what they compare) before from **your roadmap**.  
- Use **review text** and **Q&A on listings** as qualitative demand/risk signals, not only star averages.  
- If you cannot access the real market, use a **public analogue** (another brand’s listing, another category) and state the limitation honestly in “More discovery.”
